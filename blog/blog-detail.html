"The kiln that waits: inside Murmur Atelier's six-month firing cycle"

Ravi Mehta
Co-founder · Lahar Hub
Vol. 07 May 28, 2026 16 min read

It begins with a single block of clay, roughly the weight of a newborn, slapped onto a spinning wheel in a stone-floored workshop two hours north of Kyoto. The air smells of damp earth and charcoal. Outside, it is November. Inside, Yoshiko Murata is already thinking about June.

Murmur Atelier is not a place that answers to calendars. The studio fires its wood kiln only twice a year — once in late spring and once in early winter. Each firing cycle, from prep to unload, takes roughly six months. That means the cup you buy in December was designed the previous winter, thrown in spring, trimmed in summer, bisque-fired in autumn, glazed in October, and wood-fired in November. It will arrive at your door carrying nearly a full calendar year of attention.

"People ask why our prices don't drop," says Yoshiko, who co-founded the studio with her brother Kenji. "I tell them: we cannot make more than two hundred pieces per firing. That is the constraint of the wood kiln. The alternative is to switch to gas and produce eight hundred pieces in a week. But then it is not Murmur."

The wood kiln as a teacher

The anagama kiln that sits behind the studio is a tunnel of firebrick and clay roughly the size of a delivery truck. Loading it takes three full days. Every piece must be stacked by hand, spaced precisely to allow flame and ash to travel through the chamber. The glaze is not entirely under the potter's control — it is shaped by where the piece sits in the kiln, how much ash settles on it, and at what temperature the flame hits that particular spot.

"You are not the one firing the kiln. The kiln fires itself. Your job is to listen and learn when to add wood, when to let it breathe, and when to do nothing at all." — Yoshiko Murata, co-founder of Murmur Atelier

Kenji Murata, who manages the firing schedule, keeps a notebook dating back to 2012. Each firing is recorded: temperature curves, wood consumption, atmospheric conditions, results. "We learned the hard way that a typhoon approaching from the south changes the oxygen flow through the chimney," he says, flipping to a page marked with charcoal sketches. "Firing 14 ended in a total loss because we didn't adjust the damper in time."

A day-by-day breakdown

The wood kiln burns for six continuous days. It requires someone awake and feeding it at every hour. The team rotates in eight-hour shifts. Here is what a typical firing looks like:

Day 1–2

Candle firing — small flame, slow rise. The kiln is brought from room temperature to 400 °C. Moisture must be driven out gradually or the pots crack.

Day 3

The body reduction phase. Flame temperature reaches 1,000 °C. Ash begins to melt onto the shoulders of pots. The team adds oak every 12 minutes.

Day 4

Intense reduction. The kiln hits 1,260 °C. The flame turns from orange to white. Ash becomes a full liquid glaze. Pieces near the firebox may partially melt.

Day 5–6

Soaking. The temperature is held steady. The potter decides the exact moment to stop. A few hours too long and everything slumps. A few hours too short and the glaze is matte.

After the fire dies, the kiln is sealed. The cooling period takes three full months. Opening it too early would crack every piece from thermal shock. "You learn patience, or you learn loss," Yoshiko says. "We have learned both."

Why this matters for the way we shop

In an era of same-day delivery and infinite scroll, a studio that produces exactly 200 pieces twice a year feels like an artifact from another century. But that is precisely the point. The scarcity is not artificial — it is structural. It is the direct result of a process that refuses to prioritize volume over character.

"Every piece that leaves this studio carries the memory of that fire," Kenji says, holding a sake cup that came out of the most recent firing. The glaze is the colour of caramel and charcoal, and it falls unevenly — thicker on one side, thinner on the other, with a single streak of ash that froze mid-drip. "You cannot design this. You cannot repeat it. That cup is the only one in existence."

For Lahar, working with Murmur Atelier means accepting that we will never have more than a few dozen pieces per drop. It means telling customers to wait. It means not meeting demand. But it also means that when you receive a box from Kyoto, what is inside is not a product. It is a document of weather, wood, fire, and time.
